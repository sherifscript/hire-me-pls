# Opening rules — the load-bearing first sentence

The opener is the most important sentence in the cover letter. Most readers decide whether to keep reading after it. The framework has two hard rules for it: it must be **specific**, and it must be **tight**.

## Rule 1 — it must be specific

The opening sentence must explain **why the candidate is applying to this role at this company specifically**.

The test: if the sentence could be copy-pasted into a cover letter for a different role at a different company without modification, it is wrong.

## Rule 2 — it must be tight

Aim for **25 words or fewer**. The opener carries the most meaning, but a skim reader abandons a run-on before reaching its payload. A load-bearing opener that is also a 40-word sentence loses exactly the reader it was written for.

If the specifics will not fit in 25 words, split them: a tight first sentence that names the connection, then a second sentence that adds the detail. Do not pack everything into one breathless line.

## What the opener must do

It must be load-bearing on the specifics. A specific can be:

- An observation about the company's situation, stage, or product
- A quantified fact about the company that grounds *why this candidate, now*
- A connection between the role's diagnosed problem and the candidate's strongest credential

The opener almost always works best when it does the third thing — names the company's real problem (from the diagnosis's section 1) and connects it to the candidate's loudest credential (the diagnosis's section 4).

## What the opener must not do

- **No rhetorical questions.** "What does it take to build a product users love?" is a non-starter.
- **No philosophical industry statements.** "The future of work is changing faster than ever" says nothing.
- **No standalone company flattery.** "I have long admired your company's commitment to excellence" is flattery, not a reason to apply. A company observation is allowed only if it grounds why the candidate is applying.
- **No "I am writing to apply for..."** The reader knows what the document is.

## Worked examples

### Fails

> "I am excited to apply for the Senior Product Manager role at Northwind Operations."

Could be any role, any company. Cut.

> "In today's competitive SaaS landscape, great product management is more important than ever."

Philosophical industry statement. Says nothing. Cut.

> "I have admired Northwind's work for years and would love to join the team."

Standalone flattery. Cut.

### Passes

> "Northwind's JD describes the activation cliff workflow-automation products hit, and the 18% activation lift I shipped at Beta Inc came from that exact problem."

Twenty-four words. It names Northwind's specific situation (the activation cliff), connects it to the candidate's specific credential (the 18% lift at Beta Inc), and makes the application self-evidently reasonable. It cannot be moved to another application, and it is short enough that a skim reader finishes it.

If a candidate needs more room — say the connection needs a clause of context — the move is two sentences, not one long one:

> "Northwind's JD describes the activation cliff workflow-automation products hit. I shipped an 18% activation lift at Beta Inc working that exact problem on a consumer surface."

Two tight sentences carry the same load as the failed 44-word run-on, and the reader stays with them.

## How to build a passing opener

1. Open the diagnosis. Read section 1 (the problem the team is hiring to fix) and section 4 (the candidate's loudest credential).
2. Draft a sentence that states the company's problem in plain terms.
3. Connect it to the credential with a clause that makes the link explicit.
4. Count the words. If it runs past 25, split it into two sentences rather than trimming the specifics out.
5. Apply the operational voice test (`voice-anchor.md`): could this exact sentence appear elsewhere? If yes, the specific is not specific enough.

## After the opener

The opener earns the reader's attention. The body spends it: one paragraph on the most relevant arc of the candidate's work (told plainly, as description not pitch), optionally a second on a complementary arc or the objection-handling (see `objections-and-close.md`), and a short warm close. Keep the whole letter between 200 and 300 words. The opener does the most work, but it is not licensed to be a run-on: a tight opener followed by a tight second sentence beats one long line every time.
