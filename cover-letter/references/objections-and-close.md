# Objections and the close — the two things most cover letters skip

A recruiter reads a cover letter doing one thing: risk assessment. Two parts of the letter speak directly to that, and most AI-written letters skip both. This file covers them.

## Part 1 — naming the obvious objection

### Why this matters

Every candidacy that is not a perfect on-paper match has a visible risk. The recruiter sees it in the first ten seconds of the CV, before they reach the cover letter. The question is not whether they will notice it. They already have. The question is whether the letter addresses it or pretends it is not there.

A letter that names the risk and resolves it reads as confident. A letter that ignores a risk the recruiter is already looking at reads as either unaware or evasive, and both are worse than the risk itself.

### What counts as an obvious objection

Only flag a risk that a recruiter would actually pause on. The common ones:

- **Career change.** The candidate is moving between functions (engineering to product, journalism to comms, academia to industry).
- **Industry switch.** Same role, different sector, where domain knowledge is part of the bar (consumer to fintech, agency to in-house, B2C to B2B).
- **Seniority jump.** The candidate is applying one level above their last title.
- **Employment gap.** A visible gap of more than roughly six months.
- **A run of short tenures.** Three or more roles under ~18 months each, which reads as flight risk.
- **Geographic mismatch.** Applying to a role in a market the candidate has not worked in, where work authorization or relocation is a live question.
- **Overqualification.** The candidate is applying below their last level, which makes the recruiter wonder why, and whether they will leave.

If the candidacy has none of these — the CV is a clean, obvious match — **do not invent an objection.** Manufacturing a weakness to look humble is its own failure mode. Skip straight to the proof and the close.

### How to handle it — one honest sentence, then resolve

The move is: name it plainly, then give the reader the reason it is not a problem. Not a defensive paragraph. One sentence to name, one to resolve.

The resolution must be real. "I am a fast learner" is not a resolution. A resolution is a fact that genuinely reduces the risk: prior exposure the CV does not make obvious, a transferable result, a deliberate reason for the move.

### Worked examples

**Industry switch (B2C to B2B):**

> "My product work has been on consumer surfaces, and Northwind is B2B, so the fair question is whether the activation problem transfers. It does: the activation cliff is a funnel problem before it is a market problem, and the funnel mechanics I worked at Beta Inc are the same ones Northwind's onboarding describes."

**Employment gap:**

> "I took eight months off in 2025 to care for a family member, which the dates on my CV make visible. I used part of it to ship two open-source contributions to the Amplitude SDK, so the gap was not a gap in the work."

**Seniority jump:**

> "This role is a step up in title from my current one. What makes it not a stretch is scope: I have run the activation roadmap as the de facto owner for two years, including the OKRs and the cross-functional calls, without the title attached."

**Short tenures:**

> "My last two roles were both under eighteen months, which I would want to explain. Both were fixed-term startup contracts that ended when the funding round did, not departures I chose. I am looking for somewhere to stay and compound."

### Where it goes in the letter

The objection paragraph is the third paragraph — after the opener and the strongest-proof paragraph. By then the reader has seen the candidate is credible, so the objection lands as a confident aside rather than a confession. Never lead with it.

If the objection is the single biggest fact about the candidacy, it can share the second paragraph with the proof. Use judgement. It must never be the opener.

## Part 2 — the close

### What the close must do

The close does two things and stops: it states one concrete next step, and it leaves a warm, human final impression. Two sentences, three at most.

### The next step

Concrete means actionable. "I would welcome the opportunity to discuss further" is not a next step, it is a sentence shaped like one. A real next step gives the reader something specific:

- An offer to send something: a portfolio, a work sample, a reference.
- A note on availability and start timing.
- A simple statement that the candidate would value a conversation, naming what it would cover.

### Warmth without flattery

The last sentence is the one place sincerity is not optional. A letter that ends on pure logistics reads as cold. But warmth is not flattery — see [`voice-anchor.md`](./voice-anchor.md). Warmth is a real, specific note about why this role is worth wanting. Flattery is generic praise of the company.

### Worked examples

**Passes:**

> "I would be glad to walk through the activation work in more detail, and I can share the experiment writeups if useful. The onboarding problem Northwind is hiring for is the one I find most worth doing, which is the real reason I am writing."

> "I am available to start within four weeks and happy to send a portfolio of the funnel work. This is the rare PM role scoped around a problem I already know in my hands, and I would genuinely like to do it."

**Fails:**

> "I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how I can contribute to your team's continued success."

Generic. No concrete step, no real warmth. Cut.

> "Thank you for considering my application. I look forward to hearing from you."

Pure boilerplate. It says nothing only this candidate could say. Cut.

### The format

```
Best regards,
Jordan **Park**
```

Last name in bold, nothing else styled. The signature is not part of the word count.
