# STAR+R — the story format

STAR+R is the structure for every behavioral interview story in the framework. It is used by both `interview-prep` (assembling a prep document) and `story-bank` (maintaining the story library).

## The five parts

- **S — Situation:** 2–3 sentences of context. Where, when, what was going on.
- **T — Task:** what the candidate was specifically responsible for. Not the team's task — the candidate's.
- **A — Action:** the choices the candidate made and why. This is the longest part. The "why" matters as much as the "what."
- **R — Result:** the outcome, quantified wherever possible.
- **Reflection:** what the candidate learned or would do differently.

## Why the Reflection line exists

STAR is the standard. STAR+R adds the Reflection because it is what separates a polished answer from a rote one. Senior interviewers specifically probe for it — "what would you do differently?" is one of the most common follow-ups, and a candidate who has already thought about it answers with self-awareness instead of being caught flat.

The Reflection also signals growth. A story that ends at Result says "I succeeded." A story that ends at Reflection says "I succeeded, and I understood why, and I got better." The second is what gets a senior candidate hired.

## The story entry format

```
=====================================
Theme: [e.g. Stakeholder Management, Ambiguous Data, Cross-functional Delivery]
Strength: [e.g. analytical rigor, influence without authority]
Branch fit: [Research & Analytics / Product Management / General / etc.]
-------------------------------------
S:
T:
A:
R:
Reflection:
=====================================
```

## Writing each part well

**Situation** — give just enough context for the rest to make sense. Resist over-explaining. Two or three sentences.

**Task** — make the candidate's personal ownership explicit. "The team needed to ship the redesign" is a team task. "I owned the activation metric and had to decide whether to ship the redesign or delay for more testing" is a personal task.

**Action** — this is where the story is won or lost. Describe the actual choices and the reasoning. An interviewer can tell the difference between "I did X, Y, Z" (a list) and "I chose X over Y because Z" (a decision). Always show the decision.

**Result** — quantify. "Activation improved" is weak. "7-day activation went from 32% to 38% over the quarter" is strong. If the result genuinely cannot be quantified, name a concrete observable outcome instead.

**Reflection** — honest, specific, not self-flagellating. "I would have brought engineering into the prioritization conversation two weeks earlier — we lost time relitigating decisions they had not been part of." That is a real reflection. "I learned the importance of communication" is not.

## What makes a story strong enough to bank

Not every career moment is a story. A story earns a place in the bank when it has:

- A genuine decision point (the candidate could have gone another way)
- A clear personal contribution (not just "the team did well")
- An outcome worth stating
- Something learned

Moments of decision, impact, failure, leadership, or insight. A smooth project where nothing went wrong is rarely a good story — the absence of tension means there is nothing to demonstrate.
