# Hard questions — probing the real gaps

Section 3 of the interview prep document is three questions a sharp interviewer would ask this candidate for this specific role. The point is to surface the candidacy's real weak spots before the interview, so the candidate has thought through an answer.

## What a hard question is

A hard question probes a genuine tension or gap in the candidacy for this role. It is specific to:

- The candidate's profile (a pivot, a gap, a level mismatch, a missing skill)
- The role (something the JD asks for that the candidate has not obviously done)
- The company (something about its stage or context that the candidate has not obviously navigated)

## What a hard question is not

- Not a generic behavioral question ("tell me about a time you failed")
- Not a softball ("why do you want to work here?")
- Not a trivia question about the candidate's resume

Generic questions are easy to prep for and every candidate has. Hard questions are the ones that actually decide the interview.

## How to generate them

For the showcase candidate (a B2C PM moving to B2B SaaS), a sharp interviewer's hard questions would be:

1. **"You have shipped consumer products to millions of users. B2B SaaS sells to a buyer who is not the user, with multi-stakeholder deals and longer feedback loops. What specifically transfers, and what will you have to unlearn?"** — probes the pivot directly. The candidate cannot wave this away; they need a real answer about what is different.

2. **"Your activation win was on a consumer surface where you could run an experiment on a million users in a week. Northwind's mid-market customers number in the hundreds. How does your experimentation approach change when n is small?"** — probes whether the candidate's signature credential actually transfers to this context.

3. **"You would be the first dedicated PM, reporting to founders who have owned the roadmap. Tell me about a time you took ownership of something away from someone who did not fully want to let go."** — probes the specific organizational dynamic of the role.

Notice that each question targets a real gap. None of them are answerable with a rehearsed generic story. That is the point — the candidate should walk in having thought hard about exactly these.

## How the candidate should use them

The prep document lists the hard questions but does **not** script the answers. Scripted answers to hard questions sound scripted. Instead, the candidate should:

1. Read each hard question and sit with the discomfort of it.
2. Decide what the honest, strongest answer is — usually one that concedes the real gap and then shows why it is bridgeable.
3. Optionally map a story from the story bank to it.

The framework can help draft a thinking-through of each, but the candidate must own the answer. A hard question answered with genuine thought beats one answered with a polished script every time.

## How many

Exactly three. More than three dilutes focus; fewer misses a real gap. If the candidacy genuinely has more than three serious gaps for this role, that is a signal worth noting to the candidate — the role may be a stretch, and the prep document should say so plainly.
